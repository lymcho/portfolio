Groupon Customer Service App

SUMMARY

Groupon customer service agents processed thousands of customer inquiries, issues & requests every day. Agents had to switch between 5 or 6 tools and didn't have quick access to frequent features. Pizza, an internal chrome plug-in, solved this problem. Pizza helped agents respond faster, keep track of separate chats, and triage issues. The tool was great but it was bootstrapped together by one engineer. I went onboard along with a small product & engineering team to improve Pizza's usability and scalability.

The Context


Pizza, the customer service tool, was an essential part of agentsâ€™ day-to-day operations. It helped agents to:

- navigate and track time for current chats

- store and send commonly-used text snippets

- quick access to a customer's profile and order info

- use a decision tree to reach a solution

A previous survey suggested most agents enjoyed this tool but encountered usability issues. They also requested more features.

Too many features, not enough room?

Most agents used only one monitor and needed to manage 2 or 3 chats simultaneously. How would we fit all these functionalities in such limited screen real estate?

It needed decluttering. But the more important task is to envision a framework to make room for future features.

Take the pizza wheel for example. It allowed new agents to find snippets step-by-step. But experience agents did not need the pizza wheel at all - they pulled up snippets by searching macros. Making the pizza wheel panel collapsible would save space for them. Similarily, making sides of the panels slidable would give agents flexibility to customize.


Agents cared the most about speed.

It was their most important performance metric. Their speed was a function of how efficient our tool was. We could measure it in two ways: how noticable the actions are and how many clicks it took to get to those actions.

The MVP version did achieved the goal of minimal clicks by exposing all the actions. But the actions lacked affordances. Inexperienced agents, especially, did not understand what the buttons or icons do.

In shadow sessions, some agents selected the entire snippet and did a manual copy/paste. They didn't notice or didn't know about the copy to chat action. Because, in this case, the icon was not a good enough metaphor and changing it to a text button worked better. I also added helper text inside of the search bar and preview to highlight functionality.

Another area of improvement was adding explainer tooltips when hovering over icons.

Devil's in the details.

In the current interface, dropdown from search bar would cover up the bottom two panels. This could be disruptive. Agents needed to cross-reference the decision tree and customer info.

The solution was simple with rearranging the elements. User test data later showed us that many agents noticed and appreciated this change.

As part of the user test, I built a coded prototype using javascript to simulate this interaction.

Lean Design System

Groupon's current design system didn't have font styles that would fit our needs. This app required a set of much more compact UI elements. I put together a lean design system. The system included a small set of styles and components. Engineers used this as front-end code out-of-box and leveraged it for future iterations.
