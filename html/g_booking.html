Groupon as a Bookable Marketplace



SUMMARY

Groupon was repositioning from a discount site to a bookable marketplace. It was a multi-year effort. I worked on several areas of booking as an individual contributor at first in 2019. In early 2021, I started leading this effort. I did IC work while project managing 2 other senior designers and a junior designer.


DATE

2019-present


Role

Lead Product Designer




Why bookable marketplace?


THE USER PROBLEM

Customers loved discounts and they came to Groupon for it. But after buying deals, they found it hard to redeem them. It required calling up the merchant and scheduling was difficult. Data showed that customers wouldn’t buy a new deal until they had used the previous one. Customers needed an easier way to check availabilities and book online.

THE INVENTORY PROBLEM

We didn't have enough bookable inventory. Because most local merchants on Groupon didn't have the skills or time to manage bookings.

OUR GOAL

We needed to evolve our current consumer experience. To keep customers, we had to deliver not only value but also convenience. At the same time, we needed to expand our bookable inventory.


How do we enable booking without impacting conversion?

Groupon’s deal page was the product of 10+ years of iterative design and development. It intended to flow customers through limited options toward a simple path to buy. Any extra element would impact conversion and should be carefully considered. How would we insert booking here?

We took an iterative approach. As MVP, we placed a jump-off on the page. It was prominent enough that high-intent users wouldn't miss it. These customers would understand they could make bookings with their purchases. At the same time, we wanted to be clear to low-intent users that booking was not mandatory. You could always buy this deal now and worry about booking later.

A/B test results agreed with our hypotheses. Customers liked to check availabilities as they were making purchase decisions. We also learned that customers converted better when deals had high availability. This proved the importance of increasing bookable inventory.

The second iteration surfaced date & times on the deal page. This made booking even more visible. With this version, we also tracked the engagement rates on different areas of the module. For example, we noticed a low engagement rate on "see more date & times". This led us to trade the large call to action for a smaller one for the third iteration. The third iteration focused on optimizing messaging and a style refresh.

How do we enable users to make multiple bookings?

Bundle deals were commonly sold at Groupon.

Data showed most users who bought a multi-pack deal would book their first visit shortly after purchase. They then would book the next one after using the first one. We needed to design the best experience for that. We also had to work within limit because the existing experience where users redeemed their deals were optimized for non-bookable vouchers.

The existing orders and order details page were different on ios, android, web and mobile web. The design execution needed to account for the differences between platforms.

Designs also needed to provide detailed logic for how visits would display in a list.

To book visits for a pass, I used visualizations to help us understand a shifting booking window.

How do we get bookable inventory quickly?

Only about 25% of our deals are bookable today. To scale up bookable inventory quickly, Groupon partnered up with other booking platforms.

Groupon had a lot of partnerships over the years but not many were successful. Each integration took months of work. It required us to create, build and maintain a new customized design flow each time. The returns on investment were low.

How could we improve the cost-effectiveness? The strategy was to establish a standard integration process. For design, that would mean a standardized consumer experience we could leverage for any partners.


How to build a scalability-first booking design system?

How would we accommodate hundreds of partners? and hundreds of thousands of different products? Typically, consumer designers aimed to streamline an experience. We wanted to reduce the steps so it was easier for users to find what they want. The way to do that was to optimize the interface based on what type of product we were selling.

But in our case, we had to rank scalability over optimization, simply because we wouldn't know what type of product we would be selling.

To tackle this, I audited our current booking experiences with 30+ existing partners.

I discovered that there was a set of common selections required in any type of booking. The difference was in the way we would order or group them.

What if we isolated each step and used them as lego bricks to build a dynamic booking flow? The order of the steps could be flexible. Which steps to display could be dynamic.

There are advantages of this booking system. It splits complex processes into many steps so each step becomes simpler. With fewer fields each step, users are less overwhelmed and make fewer mistakes. While there were tradeoffs as well. This way of booking has a higher interaction cost (more clicks) than other patterns. Another disadvantage was that users can’t compare information from different steps.

At the same time, the engineering team was working on an API for partners do self-service integrations.

I worked closely with them to ensure that the API design and the booking UX went hand in hand.

Dynamic UI Display

Now that the steps were flexible, what about within each step?

Typically when we designed an interface, we would make a human design decision of which UI element to use based on types of data we needed to accommodate. However, in our case, we wouldn't know what types of data we'd get.

So instead, we allowed machines to make design decisions. At run-time, data would be fetched and analyzed for its content and structure. Then UI elements would render dynamically based on a set of rules.

Adding booking features in Groupon's deal-centric merchant platform

THE CONSTRAINTS

The current merchant platform was deal-centric. When merchants joined Groupon, the first thing to do was to create a discounted deal campaign. Some merchants might already offer booking but we didn't offer that option. How would we insert booking in the merchant world?

This required extensive cross-team efforts across product, engineering, and design.


THE KNOWN UKNOWNS

There was the "known unknown". We didn’t know how the features would work & where they would fit in but we knew who may have the answers. We needed to invite them to the table and help us fill in the gaps.

I created service journey maps to facilitate these conversations. The map, for example, demonstrated the flow of tasks needed to onboard a new bookable merchant. The roles included merchants, Groupon operational managers, partner operational managers, customer service agents and automatic bots. The touch points included consumer experiences, sales, merchant business management, merchant deal management and Groupon internal operational tools. Output of these discovery conversations then informed what we needed to build.

THE UNKNOWN UNKNOWNS

Then there was the "unknown unknowns". We didn't know whether the merchant center would shift focus from a deal-centric model. I developed a north star to showcase the possibilities so we could align on our future visions.
