Groupon as a Bookable Marketplace



SUMMARY

Groupon was repositioning from a discount site to a bookable marketplace. It was a multi-year effort. I worked on booking as an individual contributor at first in 2019. In early 2021, I took over as the design lead. I was still doing IC work while project managing 2 other senior designers and a junior designer.


DATE

2019-present


Role

Lead Product Designer




Why bookable marketplace?


THE USER PROBLEM

Customers loved discounts and they came to Groupon for it. But after buying deals, they found it hard to redeem them. It required calling up the merchant and scheduling was difficult. Data showed that customers wouldn’t buy a new deal until they had used the previous one. Customers needed an easier way to check availabilities and book online.

THE INVENTORY PROBLEM

We didn't have enough bookable inventory. Because most local merchants on Groupon didn't have the skills or time to manage bookings.

OUR GOAL

We needed to evolve our current consumer experience. To keep customers, we had to deliver not only value but also convenience. At the same time, we needed to expand our bookable inventory.


How do we enable booking without impacting conversion?

Groupon’s deal page was the product of 10+ years of iterative design and development. It intended to flow customers through limited options toward a simple path to buy. Any extra element would impact conversion and should be carefully considered. How would we insert booking here?

We took an iterative approach. As MVP, we placed a jump-off on the page. It was prominent enough that high-intent users wouldn't miss it. These customers would understand they could make bookings with their purchases. At the same time, we wanted to be clear to low-intent users that booking was not mandatory. You could always buy this deal now and worry about booking later.

A/B test results agreed with our hypotheses. Customers liked to check availabilities as they were making purchase decisions. We also learned that customers converted better when deals had high availability. This proved the importance of increasing bookable inventory.

The second iteration surfaced date & times on the deal page. This made booking even more visible. With this version, we also tracked the engagement rates on different areas of the module. For example, we noticed a low engagement rate on "see more date & times". This led us to trade the large call to action for a smaller one for the third iteration. The third iteration focused on optimizing messaging and a style refresh.

How do we get bookable inventory quickly?

Only about 25% of our deals are bookable today. To scale up bookable inventory quickly, Groupon partnered up with other booking platforms.

Groupon had a lot of partnerships over the years but not many were successful. Each integration took months of work. It required us to create, build and maintain a new customized design flow each time. The returns on investment were low.

How could we improve the cost-effectiveness? The strategy was to establish a standard integration process. For design, that would mean a standardized consumer experience we can leverage for any partners.


How do we build a scalability-first booking design system?

How would we accommodate hundreds of partners? and hundreds of thousands of different products? Typically, consumer designers aimed to streamline an experience. We wanted to reduce the steps so it was easier for users to find what they want. The way to do that was to optimize the interface based on what type of product we were selling.

But in our case, we had to rank scalability over optimization, simply because we wouldn't know what type of product we would be selling.

To tackle this, I audited our current booking experiences with 30+ existing partners. I discovered that there was a set of common selections required in any type of booking. The difference was in the way we order or group them.

What if we isolate each step and use them as lego bricks to build a dynamic booking flow? The order of the steps could be flexible. Which steps to display could be dynamic.

Dynamic UI Display

Typically when we designed an interface, we would make a human decision of which UI element to use based on types of data we needed to accommodate. However, in our case, we wouldn't know what types of data we'd get.

The solution was to analyze the data structure & content at run time and render UI elements dynamically based on a set of rules.

To support this, I designed a UI component library.

Adding booking features in Groupon's deal-centric merchant platform

THE CONSTRAINTS

The current merchant platform was deal-centric - when merchants were onboarded, the goal was to keep them laser-focused on creating a discounted deal campaign. A salon, for example, would asked to create a deep discounted women’s hair cut introductory offer. Even if a merchant was bookable, there was no way to add full catalog of services. How could we change that?

We needed to prove the value of new booking features within our current platform, while having a vision for the future.

To achieve this, extensive cross-team efforts had to be carried out across product, engineering and design level. This means lots of people to work with and lots of dependencies.


THE KNOWN UKNOWNS

There was the "known unknown" - we didn’t know how the features would work/where they'd fit in, but we knew who may have the answers. We just needed to invite them to the table and help us fill in the gaps.

To do that, I put a service journey map in front of our stakeholders.

The map demonstrated tasks of each human roles such as merchants themselves, Groupon operational managers, partner operational managers, customer service agents as well as tasks that were automated. It shoed touch points across the consumer experiences, sales, merchant business management, merchant product and deal management and Groupon internal operational tools. It captured all the pieces that needed to be considered, designed and built for the whole thing to work cohesively.

THE UNKNOWN UNKNOWNS

Then there was the "unknown unknowns" - we didn't know how our merchant center could evolve into and whether we'd continue with a deal-centric model. With that in mind, I developed a north star to showcase the possibilities. I presented the north star in front of the entire product org together with my product partners.
