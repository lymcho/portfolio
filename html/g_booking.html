Groupon as a Bookable Marketplace



SUMMARY

Groupon is repositioning itself from a discount site to a bookable marketplace. I joined this product effort as a individual contributor initially and eventually took over leading this initiative. In two and a half years, I worked along product and engineering partners to identify and pain points and opportunity gaps. I facilitated design thinking exercises with cross-functional participants and helped develop hypothesis based on data & user research. I presented proposed concepts across the product organization, delivered designs in a agile fashion to see them brought to life across web, mobile web and native platforms.


DATE

2019-present


Role

Lead Product Designer




Why bookable marketplace?


THE USER PROBLEM

Customers love discounts and they came to Groupon for it. But after buying deals, they found it hard to redeem them - it required calling up the merchant and scheduling is difficult. Data showed that customers won’t buy a new deal until they’ve used the previous one. Customers need an easier way to check availabilities and book online.

THE MERCHANT PROBLEM

Merchants hated discounts but discounts brought in customers. Merchants' strategy was to deep discount one or a few doorbuster products, in hope of converting first time customers into repeat customers. This model was effective yet created some problems. Many merchants did not have the technical skills or time to effectively manage their bookings or learning new tools. How do we enable merchants to offer booking, in the easiest way possible?

Our approach

On the consumer side, we needed to evolve our current experience to be a destination of not just value but also convenience. We needed to rely less on doorbuster deals, instead pivoted to a lot more bookable products, either by making existing deals bookable, and by acquiring new merchants who already offer booking.


How do we enable booking without impacting conversion?

Adding bookability impacts the entire e-commerce funnel of discovery, conversion, transaction and post-purchase experience. My designs covered all of these areas but one of the most challenging piece was booking experience on the deal page. When consumers arrives here, they are deciding whether they want to buy the deal.

Groupon’s current deal page is the product of 10+ years of iterative design and development intended to quickly flow customers through a limited option purchase funnel as smoothly as possible. Any additional element on the deal page risks impact on conversion and requires careful considerations.

So we took a stepped approach - first, we want to see if there's any interest of booking. A jump-off was placed first as MVP - prominent enough that high-intent users won't miss it and will understand they can book while buying, while also being clear to low intent users who don't know when they want to use the deal that they can buy with confidence and worry about booking later.

The MVP design was very straight-forward with a focus on the messaging "Book a reservation now or later". The entire flow starts from the jump-off. Clicking on the jump-off will lead customers to another page where dates & times are selected. User can then check out with this reservation or without.

MVP was rolled out as A/B tests first in international markets, then North American by market, first in mobile web, then desktop, then native by platform. Our hypothesis was proved - customers were interested in checking availabilities as they were making purchase decisions. We also learned that, customers convert better when a deal has high availability. This further lead down the path of solving the inventory issue on the merchant side.

The 1.0 design was then rolled out - now with availability surfaced directly on deal page. In this round of tests, we had tracked the engagement rate on different area of the module. For example, we noticed that no one tapped on "see more date & times", which led us to trade the large CTA for a smaller one.

How do we get bookable inventory quickly?


Only about 25% of our deals are bookable today. How do we scale up quickly? One way iwas to partner up with other booking platforms with merchants that were bookable.

Groupon has done exactly that over the years but not successfully because of low cost effectiveness. Implementations cost too much - with each integration, a new customized flow had to be created, built and maintained.

To solve this problem, our strategy was to create a standardized process that could be automated and repeated regardless which partner we worked with.

I was brought onto this effort as the only designer to focuse on creating a standard consumer booking experience that could work for any partner and any vertical, whether it was booking a tour, or buying a museum ticket, or getting a massage.


How do we build a scalability-first booking design system?

Each partner had a unique way of presenting their products. Each product from any given partner would also require different user selections to complete a booking. Sometimes you'll need to pick the date, your party, level of access to different areas, or even seat maps. While sometimes, a date & time is all you need to complete your booking. How does a booking experience accommodate such variability of data we might get from hundreds of partners and hundreds of thousands of different products?

I started tackling this first by doing a complete audit of our one-off booking experiences with existing partners.

After rounds of ideation, I came up with a model of a modular booking system, where each common user selection attributes like date, party size could be an individual step. It's modular in the sense that, the number of steps, which steps, order of the steps could be variable based on any unique deal.

Here's an example of how different deals could be configured differently using the same set of building blocks.

To validate this model, I had retro-fited a large number of existing usecases. That gave the team confidence to move forward with this proposal.

An example of this is how Universal Orlando park booking experience is translated from their highly individualized experience to a standard booking flow.

When I started showing this to the larger team and secondary audience, everyone was receiving it at different pace with different sentiment because it was quite complex and still riddled with unknowns. It did resonate with the system architect, who was my counterpart, working on the API design.

The API design and the book ux went hand in hand. The availability attributes are the "steps" in the booking flow. The selection type of single or multiple corresponds to whether that step is a single choice or multiple choice. "writeups", which are text strings that could be customized, correspond to the titles of the steps.

Find opportunities to optimize

Even though this is a scalability-first booking syste., there are still some opportunities to optimize. An example of that is the run-time logic-driven UI display system.

Typically when we design an interface, we make human design decisions like what type UI elements to use and how to compose them we think about factors like what are the kind of data we work with. For example, how many options users are expected to select from, how many data fields need to be displayed, how many characters need to be displayed on average for a particular data field. But since we don't know what data we'll get with each new deal, we can't make human decisions each time for thousands of different use cases.

With this new system, at run-time, when a deal is rendered and data is fetched, for a given step, we'll analyze the data structure and actual content to determine which UI form factor is the best to display the data in.

How do we acquire new merchants from partners?

PRODUCT MARKET FIT

We don't know if merchants who are using our partner platform wants to be on Groupon, and if they do, what are their hopes and dreams and what are their worst nightmares. To find the product market fit, we started by doing user research.

STARTING FROM SOMEWHERE

As a team, we needed to figure out what we were going to test and what questions we wanted to ask users. As a thought-starter, I ran a session with my product manager to identify the value propositions we should offer new merchants in order to entice them to become Groupon merchants.

To prepare for this workshop so we weren't starting from blank. I had previously asked a junior designer to conduct a competitive research of similar experiences with a focus on copywriting.

USER RESEARCH

The output of the value prop working session led to materials I could use to continue our conversions with user researchers and content strategists, to prepare for a user research study.

On the one hand, I worked with our content strategist to refine the written messaging to best represent the benefits of our program, contextualized in a landing page and a marketing email. On the other hand, I proposed, to figure out what value propositions work the best, we could consider doing a conjoint analysis, a statistical marketing research technique that helps businesses measure what their consumers value most about their products and services.

How do we add booking in an existing deal-centric merchant platform?

THE RESTRAINTS

The current merchant platform is deal-centric - when a merchantS are onboarded, the goal is to keep them laser-focused on creating a discounted deal campaign. A salon, for example, would asked to create a deep discounted women’s hair cut introductory offer. No mention of adding any other services or adding availability. Working with a deal-centric platform from its core, how do we work in features that do the opposite?

To tackle this challenge, the key was to prove out the values of these new features by incrementally building a minimal viable product that works within the current system, but also have a vision for what the future could be.


THE FEAR

To achieve this, extensive cross-team efforts have to be carried out on the product, engineering and design level. In the past, other projects had encounter similar challenges, and lots of them had failed or chosen the easy and obvious way out. Common reason is fear of unknown - Unknown people to work with, unknown tech and timeline dependencies. To increase rate of success, I believe, is to not to be afraid of the unknown - just start by building a relationship and find common goals.


THE KNOWN

That’s the way I approached it. To build incremental features, it was known unknown, we didn’t know how the features would work/where they'd fit in, but we knew who may have the answers. We just needed to invite them to the table and help us fill in the gaps.

In this example, I had drawn out a service journey map, which we put in in front of all of our stakeholders. This had helped us get answers as we were trying to figure out how to enable bookability in our existing system so merchants make their existing deals bookable.

The map demonstrated tasks of each human roles such as merchants themselves, Groupon operational managers, partner operational managers, customer service agents as well as tasks that were automated. It has touch points across the consumer experiences, sales, merchant business management, merchant product and deal management and Groupon internal operational tools. It captured various pieces that needed to be considered, designed and built for the whole thing to work cohesively.

THE UNKNOWN

To envision the future state of Groupon's merchant platform, it’s unknown unknowns. We didn’t know whether a different service-centric model was necessarily going to out perform the deal-centric model. I developed a north star, that's built on the base of the north star developed by the merchant platform designers. The goal of the north star was to inspire innovative changes, and to showcase possibilities that were not obvious solutions. I presented the north star in front of the entire product org together with my product partners.
